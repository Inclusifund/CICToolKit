Common Questions

Everything you need to know about CICs

What is a Community Interest Company (CIC)?

A CIC is a special type of limited company designed for social enterprises that want to use their profits and assets for the public good. CICs are regulated by the CIC Regulator and must pass the "community interest test" and maintain an "asset lock" to prevent private benefit.

What is the asset lock requirement?

The asset lock ensures that a CIC's assets (including profits) are used for community benefit, not private gain. When a CIC is dissolved, its assets must be transferred to another asset-locked body (another CIC, charity, or community organization), never to members or shareholders.

How long does CIC registration take?

CIC registration typically takes 2-4 weeks after submitting your application to Companies House. The CIC Regulator reviews your community benefit statement (CIC36) to ensure it meets eligibility requirements. Using our tools can help you avoid common rejections and speed up the process.

Can a CIC make a profit?

Yes! CICs can generate profits through trading, grants, donations, or mixed revenue models. However, profits must be reinvested into the community purpose, not distributed to members. There are strict limits on dividend payments to investors (currently capped at 35% of distributable profits).

What are the annual reporting requirements for CICs?

CICs must file a CIC34 Community Interest Company Report annually, in addition to standard Companies House filings (accounts and confirmation statement). The CIC34 demonstrates how you've delivered community benefit during the year. Failure to file can result in penalties or dissolution.
